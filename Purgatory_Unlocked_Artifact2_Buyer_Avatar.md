## Part 1: Primary Buyer Avatar — The Seven-Element Profile

### Element 1: WHO

**Name:** Mark and Sarah Caldwell
**Age Band:** 42–55 years old
**Income Band:** $175,000–$350,000+ household income
**Home Metro:** Dallas/Fort Worth, Houston, Phoenix, or Albuquerque (the four primary feeder markets for DRO nonstop or short-drive access)
**What They Drive:** A Chevy Tahoe, Ford Expedition, or Toyota Sequoia — or, if they're the slightly younger affluent couple, an Audi Q7 or Lexus GX.
**Family Composition:** Two or three teenagers (ages 14–20) who are experienced enough skiers to want to go fast, but still young enough that the parents want shared family time.
**How They Book:** They search VRBO or Airbnb for "ski-in ski-out" with filters for hot tub and 4+ bedrooms. They read every review. They are willing to pay a significant premium over a hotel for the right property. They book 3–6 months in advance for spring break or the holiday window. They have used points or bonuses to offset the cost before, but they don't let that drive the decision.

### Element 2: AWAY-FROM (The Real Pain)

*In their own words, these are the frustrations that make them look for a different option:*

**The Mega-Resort Nightmare.** Mark and Sarah have done Vail, Breckenridge, and Keystone. They know the drill. "Avoid the chaotic I-70 travel with kids," as one Reddit commenter put it. They have sat in two-hour traffic jams on I-70 with restless teenagers in the back seat and arrived at the resort already exhausted. They have paid $200 a night for a hotel room where four people share one bathroom, ski boots are piled by the door, and the teenagers are sharing a pull-out sofa. The vacation that was supposed to be a bonding experience becomes a logistical ordeal.

**The Hotel Room Problem.** The core pain is spatial. As one Reddit parent put it: "We have two teens and either get two adjoining hotel rooms or an Airbnb with at least two bedrooms. We're all happier with a little separation and quiet time at the end of the day. It makes the whole trip better." Another parent wrote: "If money is not an issue, two rooms or a suite. I find that each of us is more generally irritable if we are constantly together for the duration of the trip." A hotel room — even a nice one — does not solve this problem. A townhome does.

**The Cookie-Cutter Condo.** They have also tried the "budget condo" option — an older unit with dated furniture, a cramped kitchen, and a parking lot view. One TripAdvisor reviewer described it bluntly: "The decor is very outdated. The wireless internet connection was about ten times slower than a dial-up connection." They are not willing to compromise on quality. They want upscale, not just "on the mountain."

**What It Costs Them.** The real cost is not financial — it is the vacation itself. They spend thousands of dollars and precious time off, and the family comes home more stressed than when they left. The teenagers retreat to their phones. The parents argue about logistics. The "family ski trip" becomes a cautionary tale.

### Element 3: TOWARD (What Solved Looks Like)

*In their own words, this is what they are actually buying:*

**True Ski-In/Ski-Out.** "Ski-in ski-out changed the game for me. I can't go back," wrote one Reddit skier. "No more waking up early to catch the shuttle. No walking in ski boots through town. Just wake up, gear up, and you're on the slope." For Mark and Sarah, this is the non-negotiable. One VRBO listing at Purgatory puts it perfectly: "Literally 25 steps from the Express Six-Pack Lift." That is the promise.

**Space to Spread Out.** The townhome layout is the product. The teens get their own floor — bunk rooms, a TV, an arcade game console, and Wi-Fi fast enough to stream. The parents get a king master suite with a gas fireplace and a jacuzzi tub. As one TripAdvisor reviewer noted: "The upstairs loft area with pool table and Xbox was a plus for our teenagers." The family shares the gourmet kitchen and the great room, but everyone has a place to decompress. "A functional layout and enough space for everyone to gather and unwind after a day at Purgatory," as one Airbnb listing description puts it.

**The Hot Tub Ritual.** After a long day on the mountain, the private hot tub on the lower patio is not a luxury — it is the reason they booked. "The pool was fun, hot tub was perfect. We can't wait to come back!!" wrote one TripAdvisor guest. The hot tub is where the family actually talks, laughs, and reconnects. It is the emotional payoff of the entire trip.

**Family Togetherness on Their Terms.** The paradox of the townhome is that giving everyone space is what brings the family together. They cook dinner together in the gourmet kitchen. They eat at the big dining table. They watch a movie in the great room. "This has been our best family vacation!" wrote one OnTheSnow reviewer. "Great family atmosphere! Everyone is so helpful and nice."

### Element 5: OBJECTIONS

**Price vs. the Mega-Resort.** Mark and Sarah will compare the total cost of a Purgatory townhome against a Vail or Breckenridge hotel. The counter-argument is straightforward: kids 12 and under ski free (Power Kids program, no blackout dates), adult lift tickets start at $9 when booked early, and the townhome's full kitchen eliminates restaurant costs for breakfast and lunch. As one TripAdvisor reviewer calculated: "Our most recent trip for 3 people, 2 nights and days in a condo a short walk from the main chairlift, including lift tickets, cost a bit less than $800."

**Flight and Drive Logistics.** The DRO airport concern is real but solvable. American Airlines and United Airlines offer year-round nonstop service from DFW and PHX. Purgatory's official shuttle service runs from DRO for $150 minimum. For families driving from Albuquerque (240 miles / 4 hours) or Phoenix (454 miles), the US 550 drive through the San Juan Mountains is itself a destination experience.

**Snow Reliability.** The San Juan Mountains receive consistent, high-quality powder. The CAIC and NRCS SNOTEL data confirm strong snowpack in the San Juan range. Purgatory's high elevation (base 8,793 ft, summit 10,822 ft) and dry climate preserve snowpack well. The resort has also expanded snowmaking for the 2025-26 season.

**Who Else Must Sign Off.** The teenagers need to know there is fast Wi-Fi, an arcade game, a pool table, and a pool. The spouse needs to know the kitchen is gourmet-grade and the hot tub is private. Both are addressed by the townhome's specific amenities.

### Element 6: TRIGGER

The primary trigger is the **annual spring break planning window** — typically September through November for the following March/April break. The secondary trigger is the **holiday reunion window** — families coordinating a multi-family Christmas or New Year's trip in August through October.

The emotional trigger beneath the logistical one is the **"last years at home" realization**: "The kids are getting older. This might be one of the last ski trips before they go to college. I want it to be perfect." This is the urgency that converts a browser into a booker.
